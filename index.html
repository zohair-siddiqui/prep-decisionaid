Worried about cost?

Most people pay $0 for PrEP. Under the Affordable Care Act, most private insurance, Medicaid, and Medicare must cover PrEP (medication, visits, and labs) with no copay. If you're uninsured or get charged anyway, assistance programs can cover most or all of the cost.

What about cost?

The list prices you might see online are not what most people pay. Here's how it actually works.

If you have insurance

Most private plans, Medicaid, and Medicare are required to cover PrEP, including your medication, clinic visits, and lab tests, with no copay or deductible, because it's a federally protected preventive service.

Note: some people still get billed by mistake. If that happens, you have the right to appeal, and a clinic PrEP navigator can help.

If you're uninsured or underinsured

  • Free-medication programs: the makers of these drugs offer programs that provide PrEP at no cost for people under a certain income (no Social Security number required for some).
  • Copay assistance: if you have private insurance but still face costs, manufacturer copay programs can cover a large share of out-of-pocket medication costs. (These usually don't apply to Medicaid/Medicare.)
  • Clinics & health centers: community health centers, Ryan White clinics, and local health departments offer PrEP (including labs and visits) on sliding scales that can reach $0.

Most assistance programs cover the medication but not always your visits and labs. A clinic can help you cover those too.

The simplest first step

Ask any sexual-health clinic about their PrEP navigation services. Navigators handle this paperwork every day, know which program fits your situation, and can often get your cost to $0, before you ever assume you can't afford it.

Common questions

Q What if I miss a dose or shot?

Daily pills: take it as soon as you remember. Missing doses lowers protection, especially if you were assigned female at birth. Call your provider if you've missed more than 4 pills in your prescription. Apretude: there's a ±1 week window around your due date. Yeztugo: there's a ±2 week window. If you're late for a shot, call your clinic.

Q Can I stop PrEP? What happens?

Yes, anytime, but tell your provider before stopping. The medicine takes time to leave your body, so continue HIV testing for up to a year after your last doses, and switch to another prevention method if you're still at risk. You can restart whenever you need to.

Q Who can take PrEP?

Anyone age 12 or older, weighing at least ~77 lbs (35 kg), who is HIV-negative at the start and tested before each dose. One exception: Descovy isn't approved for people assigned female at birth who are at risk through vaginal sex. Truvada, Apretude, and Yeztugo are all options.

Q Does PrEP prevent other STIs?

No. PrEP only prevents HIV. Condoms and other methods protect against other sexually transmitted infections. Ask your provider about combining them.

Q Will other medicines interfere?

A few drugs can interact with PrEP. Bring a full list of everything you take, including supplements, to your visit so your provider can check.

Q Is the newest shot harder to get covered?

Sometimes. Because Yeztugo is new and expensive, some insurers and pharmacy plans are slower to cover it than the pill or the 2-month shot. If cost or coverage is a concern, a PrEP navigator can tell you which option is most affordable for your specific plan.
